Policy brief 02 / 03

Protect road capacity before removing travel lanes.

Converting a general-purpose lane can increase queues, affect local access, and shift traffic onto nearby streets. Those impacts deserve rigorous review before a project becomes permanent.

Lane conversions have consequences

Reallocating road space changes how a corridor operates. Where a motor-vehicle lane is removed, some drivers may experience longer queues or different travel patterns, particularly during peak periods. Outcomes depend on intersection design, demand, parallel routes, parking, loading, and how many trips shift to another mode.

Our standard: publish the full impact Before-and-after vehicle counts, queue lengths, travel times, safety outcomes, usage, spillover traffic, parking, and emergency access should all be publicly reported.

Use the right facility in the right place

Bike infrastructure should be placed where likely use and safety benefits justify the cost and effect on the surrounding network. Parallel routes, trails, neighborhood greenways, and off-road connections can sometimes serve cyclists without removing critical arterial capacity.

Our lane-conversion standard

No general-purpose travel lane should be removed without demonstrated demand, a public comparison of alternatives, emergency-service review, and measurable performance targets.

Balance safety with mobility

Street design allocates risk and access as well as capacity. Protected facilities can make cycling possible for people unwilling to mix with fast traffic, while removing or narrowing vehicle lanes may create driver delay. A credible evaluation should count people, crashes, access, travel time, and maintenance, not only cars per hour.
