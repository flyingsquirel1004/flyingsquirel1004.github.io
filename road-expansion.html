Policy brief 01 / 03

Capacity is freedom, measured in asphalt.

Road expansion can address specific bottlenecks and improve network resilience. Our campaign takes that limited proposition and applies it everywhere, forever.

The documented premise

A roadway has finite capacity. At a constrained interchange, merge, bridge, or intersection, a targeted capital project can increase throughput, remove a geometric choke point, or make operations safer. Freight, emergency response, deliveries, and trips that cannot shift modes all depend on a functional road network.

Evidence label: context dependent Capacity projects should be judged corridor by corridor. A benefit at one bottleneck does not establish that widening every road will permanently eliminate regional congestion.

The campaign interpretation

If targeted capacity is sometimes useful, then unlimited capacity must be infinitely useful. Under the Pave Over MoCo framework, every queue is proof that the next widening phase should already be under design.

Our preferred project lifecycle

  1. Observe congestion during the busiest hour.
  2. Widen the facility to accommodate current peak demand.
  3. Celebrate improved travel times.
  4. Express shock when land use and travel behavior respond.
  5. Commission the next widening study.

The omitted tradeoffs

Widening can require land, displacement, stormwater infrastructure, maintenance funding, and years of construction. Added capacity may improve travel at first while also changing route choice, trip timing, development, and total driving. The serious policy question is not “roads or no roads,” but which investment solves the stated problem at acceptable cost.
