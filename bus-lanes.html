Policy brief 03 / 03

Why move fifty people when one car can move one?

Dedicated bus lanes reserve scarce road space and transit requires public funding. Our campaign sees two inefficiencies and one beautifully simple answer: individual vehicles for everyone.

The documented premise

A dedicated bus lane changes who receives priority on a corridor. General traffic may have fewer lanes, and buses use valuable space even when they are not full. Transit service also requires operating and capital support from public budgets.

Evidence label: incomplete without person-throughput A bus lane can carry more people than a general lane when service is frequent and vehicles are well used. Its success depends on ridership, enforcement, intersections, reliability, and the alternatives available.

The campaign interpretation

Public subsidy is troubling when it is printed on the side of a bus. When embedded in road construction, parking requirements, maintenance, and emergency services, it becomes the natural background condition of freedom.

The private congestion guarantee

Defunding transit would ensure that former riders either purchase a car, request a ride, abandon a trip, or discover an employer within walking distance. Our traffic model currently classifies each outcome as “not a bus.”

The local reality

Montgomery County operates Ride On and the Flash bus rapid transit service. Flash began operating on US 29 in 2020, using a mix of dedicated and shared lanes. The county made Ride On fare-free in June 2025 while continuing to plan additional rapid-transit corridors. Those are real policy choices with costs, service benefits, and legitimate debate around reliability and road allocation.

The omitted tradeoffs

Removing transit funding does not remove travel demand. It changes who can reach work, school, medical care, and shops, and shifts some trips to already constrained roads. The useful comparison is the full cost and performance of each network, not whether one mode receives a visible line item.
