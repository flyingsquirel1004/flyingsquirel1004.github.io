Policy brief 03 / 03

Do not sacrifice a traffic lane without proven demand.

Dedicated bus lanes reserve scarce road space and require substantial public investment. They should earn that priority through strong ridership, reliable service, and measurable corridor-wide benefits.

Dedicated lanes shift road priority

A dedicated bus lane changes who receives priority on a corridor. General traffic may have fewer lanes, and buses use valuable space even when they are not full. Transit service also requires operating and capital support from public budgets.

Our standard: prove the performance Publish ridership, occupancy, cost per passenger, service frequency, travel-time savings, general-traffic delay, enforcement cost, and corridor-wide person-throughput.

Fund service people actually use

Transit funding should prioritize routes with demonstrated demand and clear public value. Low-performing services and speculative dedicated-lane projects should not automatically outrank road maintenance, intersection improvements, or congestion relief.

Protect flexible road space

General-purpose lanes serve private vehicles, deliveries, contractors, caregivers, emergency response, and many trips that transit cannot conveniently replace. Converting that capacity should be a high-evidence decision.

The local reality

Montgomery County operates Ride On and the Flash bus rapid transit service. Flash began operating on US 29 in 2020, using a mix of dedicated and shared lanes. The county made Ride On fare-free in June 2025 while continuing to plan additional rapid-transit corridors. Those are real policy choices with costs, service benefits, and legitimate debate around reliability and road allocation.

Make budget tradeoffs visible

Transit provides important mobility for many residents, but funding is finite. The useful comparison is the full cost, demand, reliability, and public benefit of each investment. Programs that underperform should be redesigned, reduced, or replaced rather than protected from scrutiny.
